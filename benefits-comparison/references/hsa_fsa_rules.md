# HSA & FSA Rules Reference (2026)

## HSA Eligibility — All Four Requirements Must Be Met

1. **Enrolled in an IRS-qualified HDHP** as the primary health coverage.
2. **No other disqualifying health coverage** (see below).
3. **Not enrolled in Medicare** (Part A or B).
4. **Not claimed as a dependent** on someone else's tax return.

### 2026 IRS HDHP Thresholds

| | Minimum deductible | Maximum OOP |
|-|-------------------|-------------|
| Self-only | $1,700 | $8,500 |
| Family | $3,400 | $17,000 |

A plan must meet both thresholds to qualify as an HDHP. Plan names like "HDHP 1700" typically reference the individual deductible — always confirm against the SBC.

### 2026 HSA Contribution Limits

| Coverage | Annual limit (employee + employer combined) |
|----------|---------------------------------------------|
| Self-only | $4,400 |
| Family | $8,750 |
| Catch-up (age 55+) | +$1,000 additional |

## What Counts as Disqualifying Coverage

These disqualify an HSA even if the HDHP enrollment is otherwise valid:

- Any non-HDHP health plan that covers the employee (including a spouse's plan if it covers the employee)
- Medicare Part A or B enrollment
- TRICARE (unless also enrolled in TRICARE with HDHP)
- General-purpose Health Care FSA — at the employee's employer **or** a spouse's employer if the FSA covers the employee
- Health Reimbursement Arrangement (HRA) — unless it is an HDHP-compatible (post-deductible) HRA

### What Does NOT Disqualify an HSA

- Spouse enrolled in a non-HDHP — **as long as that plan does not cover the employee**
- Stand-alone dental or vision plans (any tier)
- Limited-Purpose FSA (covers only dental and vision)
- Accident, disability, or long-term care insurance
- Workers' compensation coverage
- Coverage for a specific disease or fixed indemnity coverage

## The Spousal Coverage Rule — Most Misunderstood

**Scenario:** Employee is on HDHP. Spouse starts a job with a PPO (non-HDHP).

| Spouse's plan covers... | Employee HSA eligible? |
|------------------------|----------------------|
| Spouse only | ✅ Yes — no impact |
| Spouse + Employee | ❌ No — disqualifying |
| Spouse + Children | ✅ Yes — children's coverage doesn't affect employee |

**Action required when spouse has a non-HDHP:** Ensure the employee is NOT added to the spouse's medical plan. If the employee wants to keep HSA eligibility, enroll employee as self-only on their own HDHP.

---

## The Spousal FSA Trap

If the spouse has an active general-purpose Health Care FSA at their employer, it disqualifies the employee's HSA — even if the FSA is in the spouse's name and only covers the spouse.

**Why:** The IRS treats a spouse's general-purpose FSA as extending to the family unit, creating "other coverage" for the employee.

**Fix:** The spouse must **waive** (decline) the Health Care FSA enrollment at their employer.

**Timing:** The waiver must be in effect before the employee begins contributing to the HSA. If the spouse starts a new job mid-year and would enroll in an FSA at that time, the employee should immediately stop HSA contributions until the waiver is confirmed.

**Exception — Limited-Purpose FSA:** If the spouse's employer offers a Limited-Purpose FSA (dental and vision expenses only), it does NOT disqualify the employee's HSA. This is worth asking about — some employers offer it as an alternative specifically for HSA-preserving families.

## HSA Tax Advantages

1. **Pre-tax contributions** — Reduce W-2 taxable income (payroll deduction) or deductible on Schedule A (direct contribution)
2. **Tax-free growth** — Earnings and investment gains are not taxed
3. **Tax-free withdrawals** — For qualified medical expenses at any time

**Estimated annual savings formula:**
```
Tax savings = (Employee contribution + Employer contribution) × marginal tax rate
```
Use 22% federal if the employee's bracket is unknown. Add state income tax rate if the state taxes income (Oregon = 9.9%, Washington = 0%).

### HSA vs. FSA Rollover

| | HSA | Health Care FSA |
|-|-----|----------------|
| Rollover | Unlimited — balance accumulates forever | Use-it-or-lose-it ($660 max rollover or 2.5-month grace, employer option) |
| Investment | Can invest balance | No investment |
| Portability | Yours forever, job-independent | Forfeited if you leave the employer mid-year (in most cases) |

## Dependent Care FSA (DCFSA)

DCFSA is completely separate from Health Care FSA. It is **fully compatible with an HSA** — no conflict.

### 2026 DCFSA Limits

| Tax filing status | Annual limit |
|-------------------|-------------|
| Married filing jointly | $5,000 |
| Single | $5,000 |
| Married filing separately | $2,500 each |

### Qualified Expenses

Covers care for a **dependent child under age 13** (or a disabled dependent) while both spouses work or look for work:

✅ Licensed daycare center  
✅ Preschool (primary purpose must be childcare, not education)  
✅ Before-school and after-school programs  
✅ Summer day camp (not overnight camp)  
✅ In-home caregiver (babysitter, nanny) — must provide their SSN/TIN for your taxes  
✅ Au pair expenses  

❌ Overnight camps  
❌ K-12 tuition (preschool is fine; kindergarten is borderline — confirm with tax advisor)  
❌ Informal care by a grandparent or relative who won't report the income or provide a TIN  
❌ Care for children age 13 or older (unless disabled)  

**Grandparent rule:** If a grandparent informally watches the children, the care typically does NOT qualify for DCFSA because the grandparent won't issue a receipt with their TIN (and may not claim the income). If the grandparent is willing to operate as a formal caregiver (provide their SSN, claim the income), it can qualify.

### Mid-Year DCFSA Enrollment

An employee can elect DCFSA mid-year if they have a qualifying life event. Starting a new daycare or preschool arrangement qualifies as a QLE in most plans. Confirm with HR.

---

## Health Care FSA (General-Purpose HCFSA)

Use when the employee is NOT eligible for an HSA (e.g., enrolled in a PPO or non-HDHP):

- **2026 employee limit:** $3,300
- Covers medical, dental, vision out-of-pocket expenses
- Use-it-or-lose-it (up to $660 optional rollover or 2.5-month grace period — employer's choice)
- **Cannot be used simultaneously with an HSA** (general-purpose FSA disqualifies HSA)

## Interaction Summary Table

| Situation | HSA OK? | Notes |
|-----------|---------|-------|
| Employee on HDHP only | ✅ | Full contributions |
| Employee on HDHP + spouse on PPO (spouse not covering employee) | ✅ | Most common dual-employer setup |
| Employee on HDHP + enrolled in spouse's PPO | ❌ | Drop employee from spouse's plan |
| Employee on HDHP + spouse has active HCFSA | ❌ | Spouse must waive FSA |
| Employee on HDHP + spouse has Limited-Purpose FSA | ✅ | No conflict |
| Employee on HDHP + employee has HCFSA | ❌ | Can't have both |
| Employee on HDHP + employee has DCFSA | ✅ | No conflict |
| Employee on HDHP + dental/vision only plans | ✅ | No conflict |
